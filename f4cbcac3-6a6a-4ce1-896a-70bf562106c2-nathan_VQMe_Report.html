Why Forcing Yourself Hasn't Created Lasting Change - The TEAR Model

Most business advice focuses on tactics—adjust your pricing, improve your sales process, find new revenue streams. But you can't sustain new financial behaviors without addressing the thoughts and emotions driving your current approach to money.

You already know what you should do. Charge what you're worth. Price for value, not just cost-plus. Make investment decisions based on potential returns, not fear. But you don't do it consistently. The reason? You don't feel like it.

That feeling—that emotion—is the step most people skip. They jump straight from knowing what to do to trying to force the action, completely bypassing the energy required to sustain it.

So you try to force yourself. Set higher prices, then immediately offer discounts. Research investment opportunities, then find reasons to wait. And it works—for a while. But rarely consistently. Why? Forcing action without shifting the underlying thought pattern leaves you split: your business needs are in one place while your head and your heart feel like they're in another. Ever felt that disconnect?

Your Pattern in Action:

When you're faced with a pricing decision or revenue opportunity, your brain immediately thinks: "This isn't that special—anyone could do this work."

That thought creates emotions of uncertainty and self-doubt.

Those emotions drive actions like underpricing, over-delivering to "prove" value, or avoiding financial conversations altogether.

The result? Revenue that doesn't reflect the actual value you create and investment decisions made from fear rather than strategic thinking.

Meanwhile, that pattern of biased thinking prevents you from accessing your incredible assets for making courageous, principled financial decisions that balance accountability with compassion.
