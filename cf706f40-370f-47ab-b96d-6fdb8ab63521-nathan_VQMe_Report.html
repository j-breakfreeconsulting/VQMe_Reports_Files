Why Forcing Yourself to Grow Revenue Hasn't Worked - The TEAR Model

Most business advice focuses on changing your actions: better marketing, smarter pricing, more aggressive sales tactics. But you can't sustain new actions without addressing the thoughts and emotions driving your current approach.

You already know what you should do to grow revenue. But you don't do it consistently. The reason? You don't feel like it.

That feeling—that emotion—is the step most business owners skip. They jump straight from knowing what to do to trying to force the action, completely bypassing the energy required to sustain it.

The TEAR model explains why your revenue efforts feel like such a struggle:

Thoughts → Emotion → Action → Results

Your thoughts create emotion (energy in motion). That emotion provides the energy to take action, which leads to your results.

So you try to force yourself to implement revenue strategies. And it works—for a while. But rarely consistently. Why? Forcing action without shifting the underlying thought pattern leaves you split: your body is executing business tasks while your head and your heart feel like they're somewhere else. Ever felt that disconnect?

Your Pattern in Action:

When you're considering raising your prices or investing in growth strategies, your brain immediately thinks: "I don't need someone else telling me how to run my business" or "This is just another thing I have to get through."

That thought creates emotions of resistance, resentment, or disconnection.

Those emotions drive actions like avoiding advice, underpricing your services, or treating revenue activities as boxes to check rather than value-creation opportunities.

The result? You stay busy with business activities but don't experience the profitable growth you want to recognize is possible.

Meanwhile, that pattern of biased thinking prevents you from accessing your incredible assets for making confident decisions while remaining open to input that could accelerate your success.

And this is where it gets expensive—not in dollars, but in who you're becoming.

Every time you resist input on revenue strategies or treat your business activities as obligations to endure, you're not just missing growth opportunities. You're reinforcing an identity: "I'm the kind of business owner who has to figure everything out alone and doesn't get fulfillment from the work."

The friction isn't about your business capabilities—it's about trying to grow revenue through approaches that activate your biases (Openness to Advice and Opinions (10-), Seeking Fulfillment (10-), Making a Difference (17-)) instead of working with your cognitive strengths (Having Courage & Conviction (100), Balancing Compassion & Accountability (100)).

Your cognitive assets give you the capacity to change your thoughts at the source. But understanding this pattern intellectually is very different from consistently shifting it in real-time when you're faced with pricing decisions, investment choices, or growth opportunities throughout your business day.

That's where guided application creates lasting transformation rather than temporary revenue bumps.
