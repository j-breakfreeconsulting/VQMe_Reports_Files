The Real Issue: Your Brain is Working Against Itself

Your VQ assessment revealed something powerful: You have two incredible cognitive assets at complete clarity—Having Courage & Conviction (100) and Balancing Compassion & Accountability (100).

When you're operating from these strengths, you're the kind of person who stays true to your values even when it's difficult. You can hold firm to your principles while remaining genuinely open to different perspectives. You naturally balance understanding people with holding them accountable. These are the exact qualities that distinguish effective leaders from everyone else.

But something's blocking access to these strengths when it comes to your career development.

The Pattern Blocking You:

Your profile shows Openness to Advice and Opinions (10-), which means you're highly resistant to input from others—especially when it feels like direction or control. You likely think, "There's no one right way to do things, except perhaps mine."

You also show Seeking Fulfillment (10-), creating a pattern where you feel trapped by obligations and expectations, thinking "I know I'm not living my life to its fullest potential, but what choice do I have?"

Combined with Respecting Systems & Authority (17-) and Making a Difference (17-), these biases create a perfect storm: You resist learning from authority figures who could accelerate your growth, while simultaneously underestimating the value and difference you could make in leadership roles.

The result? You stay busy doing what you've always done, avoiding the uncomfortable work of receiving feedback, following development frameworks, or putting yourself in situations where you'd need to be guided by others' expertise.

The Dissonance:

You have perfect clarity on courage, conviction, and balanced leadership, but your biases convince you that other people's input will limit your freedom and that you're not making much of a difference anyway.

This isn't a character flaw. These cognitive biases developed for good reasons—likely as protective mechanisms to help you maintain independence and avoid disappointment in environments where authority wasn't trustworthy. They served a purpose. The question now is: are they still serving your goal of becoming the kind of leader who naturally advances and develops others, or are they sabotaging it?

Why Changing Your Actions Hasn't Worked - The TEAR Model

Most career development advice focuses on what to do: network more, take leadership training, seek mentorship, build new competencies. But you can't sustain new actions without addressing the thoughts and emotions driving your current behavior.

You already know what you should do for career advancement. But you don't do it consistently. The reason? You don't feel like it.

That feeling—that emotion—is the step most people skip. They jump straight from knowing what to do to trying to force the action, completely bypassing the energy required to sustain it.

Thoughts → Emotion → Action → Results

Your thoughts create emotion (energy in motion). That emotion provides the energy to take action, which leads to your results.

So you try to force yourself to network, seek feedback, or engage with development opportunities. And it works—for a while. But rarely consistently. Why? Forcing action without shifting the underlying thought pattern leaves you split: your body is in one place while your head and your heart feel like they're in another. Ever felt that disconnect?

Your Pattern in Action:

When someone offers career advice or suggests development opportunities, your brain immediately thinks: "They're trying to control me. This will limit my options and freedom."

That thought creates emotions of resistance and resentment.

Those emotions drive actions like avoiding mentorship, dismissing feedback, or half-heartedly participating in development programs.

The result? You stay exactly where you are professionally, reinforcing the belief that advancement isn't really available to you.

Meanwhile, that pattern of biased thinking prevents you from accessing your incredible assets for courageous leadership and balanced accountability.

The Cost of Continuing This Pattern

And this is where it gets expensive—not in dollars, but in who you're becoming.

Every time you resist input that could accelerate your growth, you're not just missing opportunities. You're reinforcing an identity: "I'm the kind of person who has to figure everything out alone, who doesn't need others' expertise, who isn't cut out for leadership roles."

The friction isn't about your capability—you have the cognitive assets to be an exceptional leader. It's about trying to develop leadership skills through approaches that activate your biases (Openness to Advice (10-), Respecting Systems & Authority (17-), Making a Difference (17-)) instead of working with your cognitive strengths (Having Courage & Conviction (100), Balancing Compassion & Accountability (100)).

Your cognitive assets give you the capacity to change your thoughts at the source. But understanding this pattern intellectually is very different from consistently shifting it in real-time when you're faced with development opportunities, feedback conversations, or advancement decisions throughout your career.

That's where guided application creates lasting transformation rather than temporary fixes.

What Changes When You Shift Your Thinking

When you learn to recognize your cognitive biases in real-time and shift to your assets before they derail you, something fundamental changes. You stop being someone who's "trying to advance" and become a leader who naturally creates value and attracts opportunities.

Career development stops feeling like a series of uncomfortable networking events and forced skill-building exercises and starts feeling like the natural expression of your leadership capabilities. The resistance to input and guidance? It dissolves—not because circumstances changed, but because your thinking did.

You become the kind of leader who creates advancement opportunities through authentic relationship-building and skill development instead of reacting to the scarcity and competition that keeps you stuck.
