Why Forcing Yourself Hasn't Created Lasting Change - The TEAR Model

When most people try to advance their careers, they focus on changing their actions—networking more, speaking up in meetings, taking on bigger projects. But you can't sustain new actions without addressing the thoughts and emotions driving your current behavior.

You already know what you should do. Apply for that role. Volunteer for the high-stakes project. Have the conversation with your boss about your career goals. But you don't do it consistently. The reason? You don't feel like it.

That feeling—that emotion—is the step most people skip. They jump straight from knowing what to do to trying to force the action, completely bypassing the energy required to sustain it.

So you try to force yourself to be more visible, more assertive, more strategic. And it works—for a while. But rarely consistently. Why? Forcing action without shifting the underlying thought pattern leaves you split: your body is going through the career development motions while your head and heart feel disconnected from the value of what you're doing.
